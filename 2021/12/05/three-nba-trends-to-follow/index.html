Three NBA trends to follow

2021-12-05

The NBA is in the midst of a gameplay method shift. The 3-point shot is being attempted at record high numbers, and virtually every other shot at record lows. I’m interested to see how 3-point attempts (3PA) have changed in recent years and if rookies are becoming more long-ball focused. As an unrelated bonus, I’m going to look at Dean Oliver’s “Four Factors of Basketball Success” when compared to some of the NBA’s championship teams.

I’ll begin by loading tidyverse, ggrepel, and gt.

library(tidyverse)
library(ggrepel)
library(gt)

First up: 3-point attempts (3PA) per game by each team. It’s obvious that Stephen Curry catapulted 3-point shooting to where it is today. I’m interested to see how his voluminous 3-point shooting has affected the league since his Finals-winning 2014-15 season (shot 44% on 8.1 3PA).

Time to load the data.

threepoint <- read_csv("~/Desktop/SPMC 350 Files/homework/data/threepointattempts.csv")

I’m only interested in the 3-point stats. Furthermore, I have to mutate two fields. 3Ppct (multiplying by 100 to get a readable percentage) and 3Pgame (divides 3PA by games to get 3PA/game).

threepoint <- threepoint %>%
  select(Season, Team, G, `3P`, `3PA`, `3Ppct`) %>%
  mutate(
    `3Ppct` = ((`3Ppct`) * 100),
    `3Pgame` = ((`3PA` / G))) -> threepoint

I need each team’s 3-point statistics, so I’m making a new data frame called nba. This way each team has a respective line on my chart.

Golden State, was always towards the top, but Klay and Steph’s injuries in 2019-20 understandably hurt their production.

The Rockets, finished 1st in five of the last seven seasons. James Harden’s time in H-town led to an absolutely insane amount of shots taken from behind the arc. The Rockets set an unfathomable record 45 3PA/game in back-to-back seasons. With Harden now gone, their attempts have been slashed.

On to player 3-point shooting. The NBA’s young talent has noticeably modeled their game around the 3-point shot. In NBA history, 11 rookies have logged 400+ 3PA. Eight of them debuted after Curry’s rookie season. The Curry effect? Possibly.

Final metric, how do basketball teams win games? Dean Oliver identified what he called the “Four Factors of Basketball Success.” Shooting, turnovers, rebounding, and free throws. He weighted them in order of importance: shooting (40%), turnovers (25%), rebounding (20%), and free throws (15%).

Shooting is measured with Effective Field Goal Percentage (eFG%), turnovers: Turnover Percentage (TOV%), rebounding: Offensive and Defensive Rebound Percentage (ORB% and DRB%), and free throws by how often a team gets to the line and how often they make them – Free Throws per Field Goal Attempt (FT/FGA).

Oliver’s theory can include up to eight factors (offense & defense metrics). I’ll look at season stats of every NBA championship team since the 1979-80 season 3and select the four (technically eight) factors.

champs <- read_csv("~/Desktop/SPMC 350 Files/homework/data/champs.csv")

champs <- champs %>%
  select(Season, Team, W, L, Pct, `eFG%`, `TOV%`, `ORB%`, FT_FGA, `OppeFG%`, `OppTOV%`, `OppORB%`, OppFT_FGA)

A table is how I’ll showcase the top 10 Finals champs based on Dean Oliver’s success factors. I’m going to mutate and multiply the eight factors by 100 so I have a readable percentage. Then mutate a net value for each category (Net eFG%, Net TOV%, Net ORB%, and Net FT_FGA). This is so we can accurately see if the teams out-performed their opponents in each category. Lastly, mutating the final metric, FourFactorScore, which takes each net value and multiplies it by its respective weight that Oliver listed, then adding them together.

champs %>%
  mutate(
    `eFG%` = (`eFG%` * 100),           #shifting decimal point so its on a 1-100 scale
    `OppeFG%` = (`OppeFG%` * 100),
    `TOV%` = (`TOV%` * 100),
    `OppTOV%` = (`OppTOV%` * 100),
    `ORB%` = (`ORB%` * 100),
    `OppORB%` = (`OppORB%` * 100),
     FT_FGA = (FT_FGA * 100),
     OppFT_FGA = (OppFT_FGA * 100), 
    `Net_eFG%` = (`eFG%` - `OppeFG%`),      #Net values of each factor
    `Net_TOV%` = (`OppTOV%` - `TOV%`),
    `Net_ORB%` = (`ORB%` - `OppORB%`),
     Net_FT_FGA = (FT_FGA - OppFT_FGA),
     FourFactorScore = (`Net_eFG%` * .40) + (`Net_TOV%` * .25) + (`Net_ORB%` * .20) + (Net_FT_FGA * .15)) -> champsstats

I’ll select the W/L records and net statistics.

champsstats %>% 
  select(Season, Team, W, L, Pct, `Net_eFG%`, `Net_TOV%`, `Net_ORB%`, Net_FT_FGA, FourFactorScore) -> champstable

Dean Oliver’s theory worked well within the Bulls system. The 1995-96 Jordan-led team ended the season on a then-record 72 wins. Their Net TOV% of 3.0 was highest in the top 10, and a Net ORB% of 8.0 was good for 1st all time, dating back to the 1979-80 championship team. The 1998-99 Spurs excelled at getting to the FT line, and the 2016-17 Warriors shot an impressive 7.7% Net eFG% (1st all time).
